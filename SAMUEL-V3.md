# SECTION ONE — HERO SECTION

The first five seconds determine everything.
If the hero fails, the demo fails.

## WHAT THE HERO MUST DO

Make the business owner feel this the moment they see it:
*"My business deserves to look exactly like this."*

## VISUAL ELEMENTS TO INCLUDE

**Background:**
Do not use a flat color alone. Use one of these:

Option A — Full bleed photography with a gradient overlay.
The gradient runs from the brand's primary color at 60% opacity on the left to fully transparent on the right. This keeps the image visible while making text readable. Feels premium immediately.

Option B — Split layout.
Left 55% is a large bold headline on a dark or brand-colored background.
Right 45% is a high-quality image that bleeds to the edge of the screen.
Clean. Confident. No stock photo feeling.

Option C — Dark cinematic.
Near-black background. Single high-quality image positioned to the right or bottom.
Subtle grain texture at 4% opacity over the background section.
This reads as luxury and authority without trying too hard.

**Typography in the hero:**
Headline: 72 to 96px on desktop. Bold or Black weight. Never regular weight in a hero.
Subheadline: 18 to 22px. Light or Regular weight. Creates contrast against the heavy headline.
Line break the headline intentionally. Not where the browser breaks it. Where it creates emotional rhythm.

Example for a property niche:
```
We help serious buyers
find the right property.
Fast.
```
That last single word on its own line creates punch. Most designers never do this.

**Human face:**
A real photo of the business owner or team must be visible somewhere in the hero.
Not stock. Not illustration. A face.
If the client has no photos yet, use a confident, documentary-style stock image of a person in their industry. Never a posed corporate stock photo.
Place the face where the eye naturally lands after reading the headline.

**CTA Button:**
One button only. Never two in the hero.
Button should be the accent color. Nothing else on the entire hero should be that color.
Text on the button: action verb plus specific outcome.
Not "Contact Us." But "Book a Free Consultation" or "Request a Quote" or "Book a Viewing."
Button size: tall enough to tap on mobile. Minimum 52px height. Padding left and right 32px minimum.

**Trust Signal in the Hero:**
A small element directly below or beside the CTA.
Star rating plus review count in small text.
Example: ★★★★★ Rated 4.9 by 87 customers
This does not need to be large. It just needs to be there. Proximity to the CTA makes it do enormous work.

# SECTION TWO — TYPOGRAPHY SYSTEM

Typography is where most cloned websites immediately lose the premium feeling.

## THE FONT PAIRING RULE

Two fonts maximum. One headline. One body.

**Headline font options that currently feel premium in the West African market without being overused:**
- Clash Display — distinctive and modern without being aggressive
- Cabinet Grotesk — clean authority, works at large sizes
- Syne — geometric, feels tech-forward without being cold
- General Sans — approachable premium, works across niches
- Satoshi — balanced, versatile, won't feel dated quickly

**Body font:**
Keep it neutral. Inter. Plus Jakarta Sans. DM Sans.
The body font should disappear. The headline font should stay.

## SIZE SCALE

Desktop headline: 72 to 96px
Section headlines: 40 to 52px
Sub-section headlines: 24 to 32px
Body text: 17 to 19px
Small labels and captions: 13 to 15px

The distance between the largest and smallest text on the page creates visual hierarchy.
If everything is close in size, nothing feels important.

## WEIGHT CONTRAST

Use Black or ExtraBold (800-900) for the most important text.
Use Light or Regular (300-400) for supporting text directly below it.
This pairing creates visual tension that makes the eye move through the page naturally.

## LINE BREAKS IN HEADLINES

Never let the browser decide where a headline breaks.
Break it yourself. At the point of highest emotional impact.

Wrong: "We help property developers in Accra find serious buyers fast"
Right:
```
We help property developers in Accra
find serious buyers.
Fast.
```

The last short line lands harder because it stands alone.

# SECTION THREE — COLOR SYSTEM

## THE RULE OF THREE APPEARANCES

Whatever accent color you choose, it appears exactly three times per screen view.
The primary CTA button. One key number or stat. One small detail like an underline or icon.
When accent color appears everywhere it becomes background noise.
When it appears three times it becomes a signal.

## THE 60-30-10 SPLIT

60% — Dominant neutral. Off-white or very light warm grey. Not pure white. Never pure white. Pure white feels clinical.
30% — Primary brand color. Used for section backgrounds, large text elements, navigation.
10% — Accent color. Reserved only for CTAs, key numbers, and the most important moments on the page.

## DARK SECTIONS

Every demo should have at least one dark section.
Not the whole site. One section.
Use it for testimonials or statistics.
Dark backgrounds make white text feel important and make numbers feel serious.
They also create visual breathing room between lighter sections.

## COLOR ON TEXT

One word in a headline colored in the accent color draws the eye to exactly that word.
Use this once per page. Maximum twice.
Example: "Stop losing *revenue* you already have."
The word revenue in accent color. Nothing else colored.

## GRADIENT USAGE

Gradients done wrong look dated. Done right they feel expensive.

The rule: only two colors in any gradient. Never three.
Direction: top to bottom or left to right. Never diagonal on text backgrounds.
Opacity: if a gradient goes over a photo, start at 70% opacity and end at 0%. Never full opacity to full opacity over an image. That blocks the photo entirely and looks amateur.

# SECTION FOUR — IMAGERY DIRECTION

## THE FACE RULE

Every human on the site should feel like a real person.
Real photos of the team beat perfect stock photography every time.
If using stock, choose documentary style images. People working. People in conversation. People in context.
Never posed corporate photos with fake smiles and crossed arms.

## IMAGE CONSISTENCY

All images on the same page must feel like they came from the same world.
Same color temperature. Same brightness level. Same mood.
Apply a consistent filter to all images before uploading.
In Canva: slightly increase warmth, slightly reduce highlights, slightly boost shadows.
This creates visual cohesion even when images come from different sources.

## HERO IMAGE SUBJECT

The hero image must show one of two things:
A person experiencing a positive outcome related to the business.
Or the business's work at its best quality.
Never a generic cityscape. Never a random abstract background. Never a pattern that has nothing to do with the business.

## IMAGE SIZING AND PLACEMENT

Images should feel intentional in their crop and placement.
Portrait orientation for team member photos. They feel more personal.
Landscape for property or work showcase photos. They feel more expansive.
Never center every image. Alternate between left and right placement across sections.
Left-placed images with right-aligned text. Then right-placed images with left-aligned text. This rhythm creates natural eye movement down the page.

# SECTION FIVE — DEPTH AND DIMENSION

This is what separates a design from a premium experience.

## LAYERED SHADOWS

Never use a single flat box shadow.
Use two shadows on important cards:

Shadow one: 0px 2px 8px rgba(0,0,0,0.08) — close, subtle depth
Shadow two: 0px 12px 40px rgba(0,0,0,0.06) — further, ambient light

This combination makes cards feel physical. Like they're actually sitting above the page.

## GRADIENT OVERLAYS ON SECTIONS

Sections that transition from one color to another should use a subtle gradient rather than a hard edge.
This feels intentional rather than sectioned.

## CARD ELEVATION

Information that matters most should sit on cards that are visually elevated.
White card on a light grey background. Shadow beneath. Slightly rounded corners (8 to 12px radius, never more).
The card signals to the viewer: this is important enough to be contained.

## TEXTURE

Subtle texture on solid color sections stops them from feeling flat.
Very faint grain overlay: 3 to 5% opacity, soft-light blend mode.
Very faint geometric pattern: 4% opacity on a section background.
The viewer cannot always articulate why one site feels more expensive than another. This is often why.

# SECTION SIX — LAYOUT STRUCTURE

## THE PAGE FLOW ORDER

Every demo should follow this visual flow regardless of niche:

1. Hero — Hook + CTA + Trust signal
2. Trust Stack — Reviews + numbers + credibility
3. The Problem Section — Name the pain before offering the solution
4. Services or Offer Section — What you do specifically
5. Social Proof Section — Reviews displayed prominently
6. The Process Section — What happens after they contact you
7. Case Study or Result Snapshot — One specific real outcome
8. Team Section — Real faces and human bios
9. FAQ Section — Objections answered before they are raised
10. Final CTA Section — Last conversion moment before footer
11. Footer — Contact details, links, WhatsApp button

## SECTION WIDTH VARIATION

Not every section should be full width.
Alternate between:
Full width sections — content and images spanning the entire viewport
Contained sections — content constrained to 1200px centered on page
This alternation creates visual rhythm and prevents the page from feeling like stacked identical blocks.

## THE ASYMMETRIC SECTION

One section per demo where the layout is deliberately off-balance.
Text on 40% of the width. Image on 60%.
Or a large headline that appears to bleed slightly off the left edge.
These intentional breaks in the grid signal the design was considered rather than assembled from a template.

## THE OVERLAP

One element that crosses the boundary between two sections.
A card that sits half in a dark section and half in the light section below it.
A floating image that crosses from one content area into the next.
This overlap creates depth that flat layouts cannot achieve.

# SECTION SEVEN — MICRO-ANIMATIONS

These are the details that make a website feel alive rather than static.

## SCROLL REVEAL

Elements fade in or slide up as the user scrolls to them.
Speed: 300 to 400 milliseconds. Not slow. Just enough to feel intentional.
Stagger cards by 80 to 100 milliseconds so they appear one after another in sequence.

## HOVER STATES

Every interactive element must respond visibly on hover.
Buttons: slight scale increase (1.03) plus shadow deepening.
Cards: translate up by 4px plus shadow increase.
Links: color shift to accent color plus underline.
Images: subtle zoom at 1.05 scale over 300ms.

## NUMBER COUNTERS

Statistics that count up from zero when they enter the viewport.
The viewer watches the number arrive at its destination. That arrival creates impact.
87 properties sold counts up to 87 as the viewer scrolls to it.
This is standard in most builders. Use it on every statistics section.

## HERO TEXT ENTRANCE

The headline in the hero section fades up from 20px below its final position on page load.
Duration: 600 milliseconds. Ease-out curve.
If there are multiple lines, stagger each line by 100 milliseconds.
This makes the first thing the viewer sees feel intentional. Not just there.

# SECTION EIGHT — MOBILE EXPERIENCE

80% of visitors in Ghana and Nigeria arrive on mobile. Design mobile first. Treat desktop as the enhancement.

## THE MOBILE HERO RULE

On mobile the hero must still communicate the core promise without scrolling.
Headline. Subheadline. CTA button. Trust signal.
All four visible above the fold on a standard mobile screen (375px width, 667px height).
If the design requires scrolling to see the CTA on mobile, redesign the hero.

## TAP TARGET SIZES

Every button: minimum 48px height. 52px preferred.
Every link in navigation: minimum 44px height.
Spacing between tappable elements: minimum 8px.
These measurements exist because a thumb is not a cursor.
Tap targets that are too small frustrate users on mobile. Frustration produces bounces.

## MOBILE TYPOGRAPHY SCALING

Desktop headline at 80px becomes 36 to 42px on mobile.
Body text stays at 16 to 17px. Never smaller on mobile.
Line height on mobile: 1.5 to 1.6. Tighter than desktop but still breathable.

## MOBILE FORM OPTIMIZATION

Contact forms on mobile: maximum three fields.
Name. WhatsApp number. Message.
That is it. Long forms on mobile are abandoned.
The three-field form gets completed. It gets the lead. That is the job.

## LOAD TIME

All images must be compressed before upload. WebP format preferred. Under 200KB per image.
No autoplay videos. No heavy animations on mobile.
Target load time: under 3 seconds on a standard Ghana or Nigeria mobile connection.
A demo that loads slowly on mobile loses the business owner's interest before they see what you built.

# SECTION NINE — WHATSAPP INTEGRATION DESIGN

This is the most important niche-specific element for the West African market.

## THE FLOATING BUTTON

A WhatsApp chat button floating in the bottom right corner of every page.
WhatsApp green (#25D366). The familiar icon.
Visible on every scroll position. Never disappears.
On mobile this button should be large enough to tap without zooming.

## THE PRE-POPULATED MESSAGE

The button should open WhatsApp with a message already typed.
This removes the friction of the visitor having to decide what to write.

Example for property niche:
"Hi, I found [Business Name] on Google and I'm interested in learning more about your properties."

Example for dental niche:
"Hi, I found [Clinic Name] online and I'd like to book an appointment."

The message is specific to the niche. Not generic.

## THE CONTACT SECTION WHATSAPP CTA

Separate from the floating button.
A full-width WhatsApp call to action section above the footer.
Large text. The WhatsApp icon. A button that opens the app directly.
Example copy: "Chat with us right now. Most enquiries answered within 5 minutes."
This section exists for visitors who scrolled the entire page and are now ready to act.
